How EMI Is Calculated: The Formula Explained With Examples

The exact formula lenders use, worked through with real numbers, so you can verify any loan quote yourself instead of taking it on faith.

In this article

  1. What EMI actually means
  2. The EMI formula
  3. A worked example
  4. Why early EMIs are mostly interest
  5. Common mistakes when checking a quote
  6. FAQs

What EMI actually means

EMI stands for Equated Monthly Installment. It is the fixed amount you pay your lender every month until a loan is fully repaid. "Equated" is the key word: the payment itself does not change from month to month, even though what that payment is made of does.

Every EMI is really two payments bundled into one: a portion that goes toward reducing what you borrowed (the principal) and a portion that pays for the cost of borrowing it (the interest). Lenders calculate this split, called an amortization schedule, in advance for the entire tenure of the loan, and it is fixed the moment your loan is disbursed at a given rate.

The EMI formula

Lenders in India, and most lenders globally, use the reducing-balance method to calculate EMI. The formula is:

EMI = P × r × (1 + r)n ÷ [(1 + r)n − 1]

Where:

  • P is the principal loan amount — the amount you actually borrow.
  • r is the monthly interest rate, calculated as the annual rate divided by 12, then divided by 100 to convert it to a decimal.
  • n is the loan tenure in months, not years.

This looks intimidating, but it is just compound interest applied in reverse: instead of growing a deposit, it is spreading a fixed repayment evenly across a shrinking balance.

A worked example

Say you borrow ₹25,00,000 at 8.5% per annum for 20 years (240 months).

VariableValue
Principal (P)₹25,00,000
Annual rate8.5%
Monthly rate (r)8.5 ÷ 12 ÷ 100 = 0.007083
Tenure (n)240 months

Plugging these into the formula gives an EMI of approximately ₹21,700 per month. Over 240 months, you'd pay roughly ₹52.1 lakh in total — meaning the interest alone costs more than the amount you originally borrowed. This is exactly the kind of number that stays hidden if you only ever look at the monthly figure. You can check this instantly, and test different rates or tenures, using our EMI calculator.

Tip: Two loans with the same EMI can have very different total costs if their tenures differ. Always compare total interest, not just the monthly payment, when weighing two offers.

Why early EMIs are mostly interest

Interest is calculated each month on whatever principal balance is still outstanding. At the start of a 20-year loan, almost the entire loan amount is still outstanding, so the interest portion of your first EMI is large and the principal portion is small. As you keep paying, the outstanding balance shrinks, so the interest charged each month shrinks with it — and because the EMI itself never changes, more of it goes toward principal instead.

This is why the amortization schedule matters more than people expect. In the example above, roughly the first third of the tenure is spent paying down more interest than principal. Anyone planning to sell a property or close a loan early should look at this schedule before assuming they've "paid off most of it" just because they've made a lot of payments.

Common mistakes when checking a quote

  • Using the annual rate directly in the formula. The formula needs the monthly rate. Forgetting to divide by 12 will produce a wildly wrong EMI.
  • Ignoring processing fees and insurance. The EMI formula only covers principal and interest. Add-on charges from your lender change your true cost of borrowing, even though they don't appear in the EMI figure itself.
  • Comparing EMIs without comparing tenure. A lower EMI is not automatically a better deal if it comes with a longer tenure and higher total interest.
  • Assuming a fixed rate stays fixed forever. Many "fixed" rate loans in India are only fixed for an initial period. Check whether your rate is genuinely fixed for the full tenure. See our guide on fixed vs floating interest rates.

FAQs

Does prepayment reduce EMI or tenure?

It depends on your lender and your instruction. Most lenders let you choose: reduce the tenure while keeping EMI the same, or reduce the EMI while keeping tenure the same. Reducing tenure usually saves more total interest. Read more in our prepayment guide.

Why is my first EMI mostly interest?

Because interest is charged on the outstanding balance, which is highest at the start of the loan. As the balance falls each month, the interest portion falls too, even though the EMI stays the same.

Summary

EMI is calculated using a standard reducing-balance formula based on your principal, monthly interest rate, and tenure in months. Understanding the formula lets you verify any lender's quote, compare offers on total cost rather than monthly payment alone, and understand why your loan balance falls slowly at first and faster later on.
